Double-edged Technology: South Korea inundated with “Molka,” a spy camera

Technology has a profound effect — from the way we relate, obtain knowledge, and contribute to the society. Nevertheless in South Korea, more often than not, technology is abused in a way to marginalize and even terrorize women. Spy camera, used for clandestine shooting of women's bodies, is epidemic in Korea — from public toilet, public transportations, the hotel industry, coffee shop, to workplace and school, and even to living space (oftentimes by neighbours and hosts). While the cameras had been widespread for over 20 years, it has only been about 5 years since the issue has risen to the surface and began to be considered as a serious social problem: the commonly-used local phrase "molka," a term that alludes to considering the furtive filmings as men's badinage or amusement — nothing serious.
As technology evolves, the filming has become more common and the spread of *digital sex crime* got faster, which means the female victims has increased rapidly. In 2015, due to the turbulent rise of feminist movement within the country, 'Soranet' (a website active since 1999, where numerous men had been uploaded furtively filmed videos and even recruited members to have group sexual assualts) officially closed its website server in 2016. The rage against injustice continued in 2018, resulting in women-only protest on against illegal pornography taken from hidden cameras. This resulted in a few law enforcements and not to mention the social awareness on seriousness of this previously conceived "thrilling and playful acts."
The society's slow but gradual progess seemed obvious until in 2019, when the vicious chain of sex crime business cartel was eventually disclosed to the public. The public — to be specific, Korean women — were stupefied by strongly tied business, making sneakily filmed women's bodies as a means.

By law camera phones must produce a shutter sound (as in Japan) because up-skirt filming is so common. Nevertheless, the filming is not taken by phone; nowadays hidden cameras are designed to look like "non-camera," which is almost not identifiable as cameras by eye checking.

These are all spy cameras that are easily sold and used in Korea. They are camouflaged in numerous ways.
